About

When choosing my subjects for year 12 at Saint Kentigerns, I found myself at a crossroads. Having already chosen French and Accounting, I was stuck for my third option. My father suggested that I chose Computer Science, as I had ways had a “knack for computers”. Five years on and I am eternally grateful to my father for his input. While my French vocabulary still needs some work and my love for balance sheets never really came to fruition, I discovered a true passion for coding, an interest that I would love to develop into a career.

Along with this interest in coding, I have long been fascinated by the marketing industry. For this reason, I decided to pursue a conjoint degree of Computer Science and Marketing. After two years of study at Victoria University, I am currently undertaking my third year at Auckland University. Over summer, I took part in a tech internship at Healthlink, a health system integrator that enables medical practices to communicate electronically with the rest of the health sector. This was an eye opening experience that exposed me to the realities of working in the tech industry and really confirmed my desire to pursue a career in this area.

While I am most interested in developing my skills in front-end development, I am curious about all fields of development, and eventually hope to become a full stack developer. I would love to work for a leading tech company in this regard, and a company that values curiosity, passion and a desire to learn. I would relish the opportunity to work with innovative colleagues who think big and tackle challenges from a fresh perspective. My aim is to work in a environment with a highly involved and dynamic working culture, to provide an atmosphere that is conducive to personal growth.

Twitter Bot

This TwitterBot is a small project I worked on to develop my skills in Javascript, NodeJS, NPM, APIs and git. Although small, and unlikely to be the "next big thing", it was a great side project to work on to develop my personal skills.

To begin with, I did some research on creating a TwitterBot and watched a few tutorials. I started off by creating the bot to do a simple task such as tweeting out a random tweet when someone sent the account a tweet. From here, I went through the Yelp API and played around with this to get the information that I needed.

The next step was the put that information into a tweet to return to the user. This was a bug that was difficult to fix, as there was an issue with returning information from one function to another due to the Yelp API. This took me a while to resolve, but I managed to fix it by finding the information I needed in the first function and bypass needing to call the second one.

Another challenge that I had to overcome was understanding git. Git is a very useful tool, and I intend to use it with all projects from now on, however the initial learning curve for it was quite steep, and I am still climbing it. There were several times where I lost commits, or pushed or pulled something I shouldn’t have, however, eventually I managed to get my project up and running online, with the help of Git

What I enjoyed most about creating this application was seeing it live in action, most of the projects I work on are for university papers, and they don't go past the marker's desk. But this project is live and running and it is something that I am quite proud of, no matter how small.

Auckland Map

This was the first and my favourite project for the Algorithms and Databases paper I did at Victoria University. This project pushed us into learning about data structures, and creating a fun programme which displays a map of Auckland, with different roads and intersections. This project was done in two parts;

Part One:


For part one, we needed to create the basic map, parsing a file document which had Roads, Nodes and Segments. A difficult part in this process was understanding the data that we were giving, and understanding what part was a road, a segment and a node.
We were also to be able to navigate the map, with pan and zoom buttons, and allow the user to drag the map around with the mouse. In Part One, the search function was also created at basic level, where roads could only be searched if the exact street was searched for. I extended this search by making a trie, in which all roads would be highlighted when the name is prefixed by the search input, not just the exact match, however if a search query exactly matches a road name, only that road would be highlighted. This function was very tricky, as it was difficult to distinguish when the search was complete and a specific street needed to be highlighted, rather than all closely matched streets.
We were also to add functionality, where if a user clicked on the map, the node closest to the click would be highlighted. This was tricky due to having to use Pythagoras's Theorem to work out distance from the click, to the nearest node.

Part Two:


A* and Articulation Points were the main focus in the second part. The purpose of using A* was to find the shortest route between two selected intersections. This included taking into consideration one-way streets, accounting for restriction information, and finding the distance and time with the speed information to make the search prefer routes on high class roads and faster routes.
Articulation points were used so that the map could find all nodes that are articulation points, which were to be displayed as a different colours.
Part Two was an easier learning curve, as I had already worked on my code and had created a base for what was to be completed. However the A* work was difficult as the information that needed to be collected was vast and difficult to process.

Overall, I really enjoyed working on the map as it helped me understand a lot of different data structures and algorithms.
